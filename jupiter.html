Jupiter — Planet

Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system and one of the most fascinating worlds orbiting the Sun. Known as a gas giant, Jupiter is massive, powerful, and filled with dramatic storms and swirling clouds. Although it cannot support life as we know it, Jupiter plays a crucial role in protecting and shaping the solar system

Exploring jupiter in Depth

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and is located between Mars and Saturn. It is so large that all the other planets in the solar system could fit inside it. With a diameter of about 143,000 kilometers (88,800 miles), Jupiter is more than 11 times wider than Earth. Its mass is more than twice that of all the other planets combined. Despite its enormous size, Jupiter is made mostly of gases and does not have a solid surface like Earth. Composition and Structure

Jupiter is mainly composed of hydrogen and helium, similar to the Sun. Beneath its thick clouds lies a deep atmosphere that gradually becomes denser. Scientists believe that deep inside Jupiter, the pressure is so intense that hydrogen turns into a liquid metal form. At the center, there may be a small rocky core, but it is surrounded by extremely hot and compressed material. Because Jupiter spins very quickly—once every 10 hours—it has a noticeable bulge around its equator. This fast rotation also causes strong winds and powerful storms in its atmosphere. The Great Red Spot and Storms

One of Jupiter’s most famous features is the Great Red Spot, a massive storm that has been raging for more than 300 years. This giant storm is larger than Earth and produces extremely strong winds. Jupiter’s atmosphere is filled with colorful bands of clouds that move in opposite directions, creating turbulence and swirling patterns. The planet’s clouds are made of ammonia crystals and other chemicals, giving Jupiter its beautiful stripes of white, orange, brown, and red.

Moons of Jupiter Jupiter has the largest number of moons in the solar system, with more than 90 confirmed moons. The four largest are called the Galilean moons—Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto—discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610.
