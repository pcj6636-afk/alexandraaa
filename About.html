About Us

The discovery of planets is a story that stretches across thousands of years and shows how human understanding of the universe hassteadily evolved from simple observation to advanced scientific exploration. In ancient times, people looked up at the night sky and noticed that while most stars stayed fixed in place, a few bright.

objects slowly moved across the heavens; these “wandering stars” were later called planets. Early civilizations such as the Babylonians, Egyptians, Greeks, and Chinese carefully recorded these movements and connected them to calendars, seasons, and even human судьбы, mixing science with mythology. For centuries, the Earth-centered (geocentric) model dominated thinking, with scholars like Ptolemy describing complex systems of circles to explain planetary motion. This view was challenged during the Renaissance, when Nicolaus Copernicus proposed a Sun-centered (heliocentric) system, fundamentally changing how people understood the structure of the solar system. Soon after, Galileo Galilei used a telescope to observe Jupiter’s moons and the phases of Venus, providing strong evidence that not everything revolved around Earth.

These discoveries sparked a scientific revolution and encouraged others, such as Johannes Kepler and Isaac Newton, to develop laws that accurately
described planetary motion and gravity. As telescopes improved, astronomers discovered new planets like Uranus and Neptune, expanding the known limits
of the solar system and revealing that it was far larger and more complex than previously imagined. In the 20th century, the age of space exploration
brought spacecraft that could travel to planets, take close-up images, and analyze their atmospheres, surfaces, and magnetic fields. Missions like Voyager,
Mariner, and Cassini transformed planets from distant points of light into detailed, dynamic worlds with storms, volcanoes, rings, and moons.


The understanding of planets dramatically changed during the Renaissance period. In 1543, Nicolaus Copernicus introduced the heliocentric model, which proposed that the Sun, not Earth, was at the center of the solar system. According to his theory, Earth and the other planets revolved around the Sun. Although this idea was controversial and challenged religious teachings at the time, it laid the foundation for modern astronomy. Later, Galileo Galilei improved the telescope and made important discoveries, including the observation of four moons orbiting Jupiter. This proved that not everything revolved around Earth. Johannes Kepler further explained that planets move in elliptical orbits rather than perfect circles, and Isaac Newton described the law of gravity, which explained why planets orbit the Sun. Together, these discoveries revolutionized humanity’s understanding of the solar system.
