Venus — Planet

Venus, the second planet from the Sun, has long fascinated astronomers and skywatchers alike. Often called Earth’s “sister planet” or “twin,” Venus is similar to Earth in size, mass, and composition. However, despite these similarities, Venus is a world of extreme conditions, with a hostile environment that makes it one of the most dangerous planets in our solar system. Its beauty in the night sky hides a dramatic and powerful world shaped by intense heat, thick clouds, and crushing atmospheric pressure.

Exploring venus in Depth

Venus is the brightest natural object in the night sky after the Moon. Because it orbits closer to the Sun than Earth, it is often seen shortly after sunset or just before sunrise. For this reason, it is known as the “Evening Star” or the “Morning Star.” Ancient civilizations admired Venus and often associated it with gods and goddesses of love and beauty due to its brilliant glow.

Venus is the brightest natural object in the night sky after the Moon. Because it orbits closer to the Sun than Earth, it is often seen shortly after sunset or just before sunrise. For this reason, it is known as the “Evening Star” or the “Morning Star.” Ancient civilizations admired Venus and often associated it with gods and goddesses of love and beauty due to its brilliant glow.

The planet shines so brightly because its thick clouds reflect a large amount of sunlight. These clouds are made mostly of carbon dioxide with droplets of sulfuric acid, forming a dense atmosphere that completely hides the planet’s surface from ordinary telescopes.

Venus is a terrestrial planet, meaning it has a rocky surface like Earth, Mercury, and Mars. It has a solid crust, a mantle, and likely a metallic core. Venus is nearly the same size as Earth, with a diameter about 95% that of our planet. Its gravity is also similar, meaning a person standing on Venus would weigh almost the same as they do on Earth.

However, the similarities mostly end there. Venus rotates very slowly and in the opposite direction of most planets. This is called retrograde rotation. On Venus, the Sun rises in the west and sets in the east. One day on Venus (one full rotation) takes about 243 Earth days, which is longer than a Venusian year, which lasts about 225 Earth days.
